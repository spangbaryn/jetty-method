Why building with AI feels like magic—until it doesn't.

You've probably experienced it. You open Claude or ChatGPT, describe what you want to build, and within minutes you have working code. It's exhilarating. You think: "I could build anything."

Then you try to add a feature. Or fix a bug. Or make it work with real data. Suddenly, the magic disappears. The AI gives you code that breaks what was working. You spend hours debugging, going in circles, watching your project slowly fall apart.

Why most AI-assisted projects fail

The problem isn't the AI. The problem is that "vibe coding"—the practice of describing what you want and hoping for the best—doesn't scale. It works for demos and prototypes. It falls apart for real software.

Real software needs to handle errors gracefully. It needs to work when users do unexpected things. It needs to be maintainable over time. None of these things happen by accident.

The missing methodology

Professional software engineers have spent decades developing methodologies for building reliable software. They write tests. They handle errors. They think about edge cases. They refactor and improve.

What if you could apply those same principles—but with AI doing the heavy lifting? What if there was a framework that guided you from "vibe coding" to production-ready software?

That's what The Jetty Method provides.
